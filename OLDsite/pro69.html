PROPOSAL for a series of American Poets And Recording Technology

This series of at least thirteen, perhaps twenty-six, 30-minute radio programs will be devoted to the development of American Poets' involvement with the technologies of recording. The opening programs would be devoted to the earliest recordings, even if they are in acoustically poor shape by current standards; and these excerpts will be prefaced by not only a history of that particular poet's involvement with recording (as well as that can be established) but with general critical remarks about his or her work and professional career. The middle programs with be devoted to the classic recordings, one program apiece, of that generation that became prominent between the Wars--T.S. Eliot, Ezra Pound (favoring the extraordinary l938 recordings made at Harvard), Wallace Stevens, William Carlos Williams, Carl Sandburg, Vachel Lindsay and Ogden Nash. Here we would endeavor to select the best poems and best renditions from the available sources, both public and private. At that point, perhaps fifteen programs down, the series will turn from documentations of poets' performances to their more intimate and exploratory involvement with recording and broadcasting technologies. We will start with the classic poets' radio plays--Archibald MacLeish's Fall of the City, Stephen Vincent Benet's John Brown's Body, and Kenneth Patchen's He Wears a Slouch Hat. The remaining few programs will concentrate upon post-war poets' direct involvement with audio technologies--to make with microphones, amplifiers, mixing boards, audiotape, samplers, and other devices what cannot made done live. Among the poets featured here will be John Giorno, Jerome Rothenberg, Charles Amirkhanian, Dick Higgins, Raymond Federman, Norman Henry Pritchard, and Judith Johnson Sherwin. These programs will be edited, written and narrated by Richard Kostelanetz and Paul Kresh who, since they have different tastes and interests, will divide the whole series in the following way: Both will be responsible for the opening programs, Kresh will be particularly responsible for the classic period, so to speak, while Kostelanetz will be in charge of the last section. Both have written extensively about American poetry and poets' recordings. Kostelanetz, in addition, organized American Writing Today for Voice of America, while Kresh has worked as a producer for both Caedmon and Spoken Arts, personally producing l00 American Poets for the later label.
