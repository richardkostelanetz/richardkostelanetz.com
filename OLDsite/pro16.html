PROPOSAL for a book: FILM AND VIDEO: ALTERNATIVE VIEWS,
1959-1999

This book-length manuscript, currently on disc ready for
printing, is the latest installment in a continuing series of
collections of my critical essays about art.  Its predecessors
were Twenties in the Sixties (1979), The Old Poetries and the
New (1981), The Old Fictions and the New (1987), On Innovative
Music(ian)s (1989), The New Poetries and Some Old (1991), On
Innovative Art(ist)s (1982), On Innovative Performance(s)
(1984), and One Million Words of Book Notes, 1959-93 (1996),
in a broad critical enterprise that might be without precedent
in the history of American arts criticism.  As in the earlier
volumes, the essays here articulate a position about the primacy
of possibilities intrinsic in each medium.  Just as music is
primarily about the cunning organization of sound, so film and
video are primarily about kinetic images that are sometimes
accompanied by sound (and yet are subtly different from each
other).  To understand film or even video as species of
literature is no less a distraction than understanding
literature as an off-shoot of sociology; for a critic to commit
such sins is to wind up in a ditch or, alas, a slick magazine.
Another implicit assumption behind my activity is acknowledging
the importance of books in collecting together initially
scattered opinions, for I have long contended that the critic
who fails to risk publishing books is just a reviewing
journalist or, worse, an opportunist.

 Some of the essays here initially appeared in magazines.  The
last section, previous unpublished, was drawn from notecards,
each 4" by 6", that I began to type in 1959, sometimes using
both sides, where I described and judged films I had seen.
These resemble he chronicles that have appeared in other books
of mine.  What impresses me most, looking at these cards now, is
how conscientiously I treated this process of critical note
making (and how I could not marshal such seriousness today, in
part because I never became a regular reviewer of things I cared
little about, but mostly because I am now more predisposed to
forget what is not important).  Partly because I witnessed films
not normally reviewed or commonly seen (not to mention films
ignored by such putatively encyclopedic directories as Leonard
Maltin's or Steven M. Scheuer¹s), I gathered some of these notes
here.

  In the 1970s I became a video artist and a decade later a
filmmaker; so that the essays in the middle two sections of the
book reflect this experience, not only with theory but with
detailed accounts of how my alterntive films, both narrative
and documentary, were made.
