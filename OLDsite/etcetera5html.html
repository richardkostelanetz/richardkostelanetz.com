Reply from RICHARD KOSTELANETZ to American Book Review on Charles Bernstein:

Allowed only 300 words and then only one week to reply, I feel bullied already. While ABR should be pleased with such virulent responses, since some of us contributors feared the journal was becoming a sleepy, inconsequential provincial university repository, feel embarrassed, on the other hand, that only one (James Shivers) of the five respondents seems intelligent, which means they're collectively batting .200. Since one of the five plans, to ABR's credit, to cancel his subscription, consider raising your readers' average to .250, which is, alas, still well short of big-league levels. Next time, remember that magazines having more respect for their readership try to publish more thoughtful letters. (Think of Commentary, American Scholar, The Nation, etc.) Indeed, some editorial wise-guys have purportedly ghostwrote substantial letters to create the illusion of an intelligent audience.

The most objectionable sentences come from someone best left unnamed, because he is a friend who has been a guest in my house, as I have been in his: "Publication is the least of it: it should not have been written. It should not have been thought." Censorship, pal, is authoritarian; thought-control is totalitarian. The first is unacceptable; the second, indefensible. Precisely to combat both, I have served for many years now on the Freedom-to-Write committee on American PEN.

My friend finds me epitomizing "the breakdown of literary community," even though I was exposing Charles Bernstein¹s relentless factionalism, including his opportunistic erasure of colleagues. (Indeed, some might say Bernstein has been more successful at splitting apart the poetry community than writing poetry.)In misdirecting his animus, my sometime pal incidentally reminds me of used car dealers who three decades ago were angry at Ralph Nader rather than General Motors. If, on second thought, my sometime pal meant to give me an excuse to repeat an obvious truth about Bernsteinian tactics, thanks.

Much as I respect James Shivers¹ detailed reply, I should point out that he scarcely confronts the challenge of remembering Bernstein¹s poems from a fresh position. Look at Bernstein¹s own Home Page, and you¹ll notice that someone named Shivers is one of only six authors listed as having published "About the Author." For that reason alone, some editors might have disqualified his disingenuous letter from publication.

Finally, if my former(?) friend seriously thinks that political interpretations of literary moves "debases our political, as well as our literary, coinage," would he please do everyone the favor, please show more respect for his objection, by going after bigger fish, beginning, of course, with Charles Bernstein. Consider, nonetheless, that some of us might be more sensitive than others to essentially fascistic moves.

As I¹ve exceeded by far the space made available to me, know that longer replies by myself and others will appear on the website richardkostelanez.com, where you are reading it now.
